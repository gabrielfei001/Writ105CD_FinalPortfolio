About Code Experiment 1

Click the icon on the card to download the game!



In this code experiment, I built upon a previously created code experiment's story in which the player must explore a jungle and traverse through ancient ruins to survive being stranded. Throughout the game, the player has the freedom to make different choices that could potentially have an impact on the outcome and events of the game, for example whether or not a player chooses to pick up an item could potentially take away an option from their future choices. Unlike the previous code experiment which used Inky, I opted to use Ren'Py to incorporate audio and visual elements in hopes of creating a more immersive environment for the player. I wanted the player to feel as though they themselves were transported to this jungle and ruin area, that when faced with dangerous, fearful situations, they too could feel that feeling of shock and awe, but if anything I hoped to capture the player's attention all the way to the end of the game.

Because I ended up using Ren'Py for this code experiment, I had to find free, open sourced assets on the web to use as visual elements in my game. And from this experience, I definitely appreciate OSS(open source software) communities much more as they offer free assets to those in need without ulterior motive, really exemplifying Brock's idea from Chapter 3 of Rhetorical Code Studies, that because there's no ulterior motive, and there's no middle man between the consumer and the creator, a tight knit relationship can be formed, I definitely feel a good relationship has formed between me and the creator of the assets I used.

Overall from this code experiment, I really got to build the foundation of what it means to incorporate narrative and character development into game design. Jenkin's reading Game Design as Narrative Architecture encouraged me to focus on the mechanics of the game (such as spaces, choices, narrative ambience), and how the structure of the game and characters I create will shape the experince of a narrative. And it was from this code experiment that I was able to build upon that narrative building foundation in later code experiments.

About Code Experiment 2

Click the icon on the card to play the game!



For this code experiment, Professor Frank challengd us to bring some life into our code by building an NPC/entity that converses and reacts with player choices or actions. So to take on this challenge, I really tried to incorporate narrative world-building and impactful player choices in this code experiment. The game itself starts off with a flashback and for the most part not much context is given as to what's happening or why things are happening, the player is just thrown into the game to discover the world as they play and immerse themselves. Each choice the player made contributes to the main character's morality and the NPC's perspective of you as the main character, these values work in opposite effect. By the end of the game, depending on what items you picked up along the way, what your morality score was at, and what the NPC's judgement score of you was, different endings would happen. I hoped that by making the game this way, the player wuld be challenged by their own viewpoints throughout the game as they tried to pick the "best" option, and maybe even open themselves up to a new perspective by picking an action that wouldn't normally align with their viewpoints, such challenges can be seen in choices like stealing food to feed your starving self, robbing someone in order to gain money, or even killing someone that backstabs you.

There was one particular reading that inspired this code experiment design idea and that was James Paul Gee's Are Video Games Good for Learning. In it, Gee talks about using empathy within a complex system and experience through simulation (p. 2 & 3), expressing how we as game designers can think about how different regions of the system are being simulated while under the guise of the system as a whole. As an example, for my code experiment as a whole, to have a sense of realism in my game, I consider and think about how the player might react or respond when faced with certain moral choices and in doing so, I'm able to simulate empathy and emotion, and get the player to invest both emotion and intellect to the player they're embodying, creating a more engaging, realistic simulation of the world.

From this code experiment, I learned how powerful the interaction between player and artifact simulating emotions can really be. As the player interacts and invests their emotion and intellect into the artifact, the game comes alive, becoming an engaging world the player can get lost in. And it's through this exchange that things such as morality, can be easily addressed, meaning we can use engaging and deeply interactive games to make a case for deeper topics that need to be addressed. Overall, I think the game is successful in challenging the player's morality in a subtle, yet chilling way, as we get to see their consequences first hand being simulated in the game.

About Procedural Analysis

Click on the icon on the card to read the paper!



In this paper, I analyzed the puzzle-platform horror game Little Nightmares from a procedural and rhetorical perspective. The paper aimed to show how through different procedural rhetoric, defined as th ecreation of systems that respond to inputs which, from them, create particular outputs, support the "message" of the game and influence the player. I broke the paper up into three parts, first describing the game itself and how it plays, then describing how certain elements within the game such as atmosphere, appeal to logic and emotion, help influence the player, and finally where the influence of the player is taking them, that is, why are we influencing them, what could be the meaning behind the game, and for this part I looked into possible theories of the meaning behind the game.

Through this first experience of analyzing a game from the rhetorical/procedural lens, I learned just how much work actaully goes into making a game so engaging that it leaves the player with a message. After having played so many games in my life, I never once thought about questions such as "What is the game trying to tell people?" or "How could different strategies to grab the players attention, help teach them better?", and so now as I look back on the games I have played or even play today, I'm left wondering about these more in-depth questions that's more than just, "Is the game fun?".



About Final Project

Click on the icon on the card to view the project!



For the Final Group project, my group wanted to raise awareness for the increasing issue of climate change and find a way to address the serious topic in a more easily accesible way. We also hoped to provide practical examples and solutions to this issue so that the player themselves could take action and become the start of a much needed change, because despite how much the topic of climate change gets blasted on the news, very little change has actually occured to stop it.

The game we created was centered around the player as the main character, walking freely in an urban city that's filled with NPCs. These NPCs offer scenarios and different lifestyle choices in accordance with those scenarios, and depending on what the player picks out of those choices, their "environmental score" will go up or down, which will give different endings depending on the score's value. There's a good and a bad ending, in which both of these endings contain NPCs that tell you the consequences of the choices you made. We wanted to make this game replayable and fun, so that whatever outcome the player got the first time, it would be engaging enough for the player to want to see the other ending, and from that learn the good/bad lifestyle choices that can affect our climate.

Closing Thoughts


As someone that has been interested in both games and code from a young age, this course has opened a new lens to see both games and code through. I've learned how to not only analyze games and think about design more from a player's perspective, but in regards to designing games, the player experience is king, it's how your message will get across, it's what the player will take from the game, and how the game might change their perspective, so prioritize the player experience. But in terms of the game itself and systems present within a game, we must think about how they work together with the player experience to produce something that's not just impactful or engaging, but ultimately enjoyable, since it is a game after all. One of the coolest ideas I got to take away from this class is the idea of coding becoming a literacy. Vee puts it in Literacy and Power in Coding Literacy: How Computer Programming is Changing Writing, that having literacy equates to holding power, those who are "illiterate" when it comes to coding will be blind about the code that is around them, different things under the hood such as data usage and distribution, cookies, all of that is out of the control of the "illiterate" user and they will be subject to the control of the literate user. It's cool to me because we can clearly see coding becoming more and more prevalent in our society, which means more and more people will be "illiterate" to code and thus subject to the power of those who understand code, which then in turn gives the skill of code and understanding it more importance. What once was for "nerds", what once wasn't considered a staple, has now become a staple to the modern literate person, and to see this shift in perspective and move towards the age of code and computers and games, is really really cool. Also, the adaptation of literary rhetorics onto coding and games, awesome...

All in all, as I stated in the intro, this course was essentially perfect for me. I enjoyed every part of it, from the flexibility and freedom of the code experiments (it was from this that I learned so many new languages), to the analysis of artifacts and systems, to even just Professor Frank's eccentric lectures and attitude, I learned so much from this class and will definitely be taking the new found game development foundation, coding skills and procedural rhetorics into my future.
